  • If 𝐀\bm{A} is an m×nm \times n matrix, then 𝐱\bm{x} is an nn-vector, 𝐛\bm{b} is an mm-vector, 𝐜\bm{c} is an nn vector, and 𝐲\bm{y} is an mm-vector.

Important

The roles of the primal and the dual can be reversed!

  • Interchange cost and constraint vectors,
  • Change minimization to maximization.

Multiplying the objective and the constraints by minus unity, the dual has the structure of the primal.

Its corresponding dual will be equivalent to the original primal.

Conversion to dual

Partition the dual vector as (𝐮,𝐯)(\bm{u}, \bm{v}), we get

maximize𝐮⊤𝐛−𝐯⊤𝐛,subject to𝐮⊤𝐀−𝐯⊤𝐀≤𝐜⊤,𝐮,𝐯≥𝟎 \begin{align} \operatorname{maximize} & \bm{u}^\top\bm{b} - \bm{v}^\top\bm{b}, \\ \text{subject to} & \bm{u}^\top\bm{A} - \bm{v}^\top\bm{A} \leq \bm{c}^\top, \\ & \bm{u}, \bm{v} \geq \bm{0} \end{align}

Letting 𝐲=𝐮−𝐯\bm{y} = \bm{u} - \bm{v}, this is simplified as

minimize𝐲⊤𝐛,subject to𝐲⊤𝐀≤𝐜⊤. \begin{align} \operatorname{minimize} & \bm{y}^\top \bm{b}, \\ \text{subject to} & \bm{y}^\top\bm{A} \leq \bm{c}^\top. \end{align}

  • This is the asymmetric form of the duality relation. In this form the dual vector 𝐲\bm{y} is not restricted to be nonnegative.

General Procedure for Conversion

The dual of the dual is the primal!

  1. The objective coefficient vector of the primal becomes the right-hand-side vector of the dual constraints,
  2. The right-hand-side vector of the primal constraints becomes the objective coefficient vector of the dual,
  3. The transpose of the constraint matrix of the primal becomes the constraint matrix of the dual,
  4. Every primal variable corresponds o a constraint in the dual, and its sign decides the sense of the dual constraint,
  5. Every primal constraint corresponds to a variable in the dual, and its sense decides the sign of the dual variable.

The Duality Theorem

Weak Duality

Throughout this section, we consider the primal-dual pair

minimize𝐜⊤𝐱subject to𝐀𝐱=𝐛,𝐱≥𝟎. \begin{align} \operatorname{minimize} & \bm{c}^\top \bm{x} \\ \text{subject to} & \bm{Ax} = \bm{b}, \quad \bm{x} \geq \bm{0}. \end{align}

maximize𝐲⊤𝐛subject to𝐲⊤𝐀≤𝐜⊤. \begin{align} \operatorname{maximize} & \bm{y}^\top \bm{b} \\ \text{subject to} & \bm{y}^\top \bm{A} \leq \bm{c}^\top. \end{align}

Weak Duality Lemma

If 𝐱\bm{x} and 𝐲\bm{y} are feasible for the primal-dual pair, then 𝐜⊤𝐱≥𝐲⊤𝐛\bm{c}^\top\bm{x} \geq \bm{y}^\top\bm{b}.

Proof

We have 𝐲⊤𝐛=𝐲⊤𝐀𝐱≤𝐜⊤𝐱, \bm{y}^\top \bm{b} = \bm{y}^\top \bm{Ax} \leq \bm{c}^\top \bm{x}, the last inequality being valid sincd 𝐱≥𝟎\bm{x} \geq \bm{0} and 𝐲⊤𝐀≤𝐜⊤\bm{y}^\top \bm{A} \leq \bm{c}^\top.

Corollary

If 𝐱0\bm{x}_0 and 𝐲0\bm{y}_0 are feasible for the primal-dual pair and if 𝐜⊤𝐱0=𝐲0⊤𝐛\bm{c}^\top \bm{x}_0 = \bm{y}_0^\top \bm{b}, then 𝐱0\bm{x}_0 and 𝐲0\bm{y}_0 are optimal for their respective problems.

 

Strong Duality

Duality Theorem of LP

If either of the primal-dual pair problems has a finite optimal solution, so does the other, and the corresponding values of the objective functions are equal. If either problem has an unbounded objective, the other problem has no feasible solution.

Proof

Firstly, the second statement is an immediate consequence of weak duality. If the primal is unbounded and 𝐲\bm{y} is feasible for the dual, we must have 𝐲⊤𝐛≤−M\bm{y}^\top \bm{b} \leq -M for arbitrarily large MM, which is clearly impossible.

Let us assume that the primal has a finite optimal solution and show that the dual has a solution with the same value (recall primal/dual are reversible). We prove that if the primal problem is feasible and its minimal value is bounded from below, then the system

𝐀𝐱=𝐛,𝐱≥𝟎,𝐀⊤𝐲≤𝐜,𝐜⊤𝐱−𝐛⊤𝐲≤0(1) \begin{align} \bm{Ax} = \bm{b}, &\quad \bm{x} \geq \bm{0}, \\ \bm{A}^\top \bm{y} &\leq \bm{c}, \\ \bm{c}^\top \bm{x} - &\bm{b}^\top \bm{y} \leq 0 \end{align} \qquad(1)

has a feasible solution pair 𝐱\bm{x} and 𝐲\bm{y}. The first system in Equation 1 is the primal constraint system, the second is the dual constraint system, and the third is the reversed duality gap, which, together with weak duality, implies zero-duality gap 𝐜⊤𝐱−𝐛⊤𝐲=0\bm{c}^\top \bm{x} - \bm{b}^\top \bm{y} = 0.

Theorem

Dual Multipliers — Shadow Prices

  • 𝐲⊤=𝐜𝐁⊤𝐁−1\bm{y}^\top = \bm{c}_{\bm{B}}^\top \bm{B}^{-1} is not a solution to the dual unless 𝐁\bm{B} is an optimal basis for the primal.
    • The vector 𝐲\bm{y} may be used at every step to calculate the relative cost coefficients or reduced gradients (to be introduced later).
    • 𝐲⊤=𝐜𝐁⊤𝐁−1\bm{y}^\top = \bm{c}_{\bm{B}}^\top \bm{B}^{-1} is called the vector of simplex multipliers or shadow prices.
  • The components of the 𝐚j\bm{a}_j’s and 𝐛\bm{b} tells us how to construct these vectors from the 𝐞i\bm{e}_i’s.
  • Given any basis 𝐁\bm{B}, any other vector can be constructed (synthetically) as a linear combination of these basis vectors.
    • If there is a unit cost cjc_j associated with each basis vector 𝐚j\bm{a}_j, then the cost of a (synthetic) vector constructed from the basis can be calculated as the corresponding linear combination of the cjc_j’s associated with the basis.
    • In particular, the cost of 𝐞i\bm{e}_i, when constructed from the basis 𝐁\bm{B}, is yiy_i.
    • Thus the yiy_i’s can be interpreted as synthetic prices of the unit vectors.

Dual Multipliers — Shadow Prices

  • Any vector can be expressed in terms of the basis 𝐁\bm{B} in two steps:
    1. express the unit vector 𝐞i\bm{e}_i in terms of the basis,
    2. express the desired vector as a linear combination of the 𝐞i\bm{e}_i’s.
  • The corresponding synthetic cost of a vector constructed from the basis 𝐁\bm{B} can be correspondingly computed directly by:
    1. find the synthetic price of the 𝐚i\bm{a}_i’s.
    2. use these prices to evaluate the cost of the linear combination of 𝐞i\bm{e}_i’s.
  • Thus, the simplex multipliers can be used to quickly evaluate the synthetic cost of any vector that is expressed in terms of the unit vectors 𝐞i\bm{e}_i’s.
  • The difference between the true cost of this vector and the synthetic cost is the relative cost.
    • The process of calculating the syntheic cost of a vector w.r.t. a given basis by using the simplex multipliers is sometimes referred to as pricing out the vector.

Optimality of the primal corresponds to the situation where every vector 𝐚1,𝐚2,…,𝐚n\bm{a}_1, \bm{a}_2, \ldots, \bm{a}_n is cheaper when constructed from the basis than when purchased directly at its own price. Thus, we have

𝐲⊤𝐚j≤cj for j=1,2,…,nor𝐲⊤𝐀≤𝐜⊤. \bm{y}^\top \bm{a}_j \leq c_j \;\; \text{ for } j = 1, 2 , \ldots, n \quad \text{or} \quad \bm{y}^\top \bm{A} \leq \bm{c}^\top.

Sensitivity of the optimal cost

𝐲\bm{y} gives the sensitivity of the optimal cost wih respect to changes in the vector 𝐛\bm{b}. If a new program were solved with 𝐛\bm{b} changed to 𝐛+Δ𝐛\bm{b} + \Delta\bm{b}, the change in the optimal value of the objective function would be 𝐲⊤Δ𝐛\bm{y}^\top \Delta\bm{b}.

  • This interpretation of the dual vector 𝐲\bm{y} is intimately related to its interpretation as a vector of simplex multipliers.

Diet Problem

Theorem (Complementary slackness — asymmetric form).

Let 𝐱\bm{x} and 𝐲\bm{y} be feasible solutions for the primal and dual programs, respectively, in the pair

minimize𝐜⊤𝐱subject to𝐀𝐱=𝐛,𝐱≥𝟎. \begin{align} \operatorname{minimize} & \bm{c}^\top \bm{x} \\ \text{subject to} & \bm{Ax} = \bm{b}, \quad \bm{x} \geq \bm{0}. \end{align}

  1. xj>0⇒𝐲⊤𝐚j=cjx_j > 0 \; \Rightarrow \; \bm{y}^\top \bm{a}_j = c_j,

Proof (of Complementary Slackness)

If the stated conditions hold, then clearly (𝐲⊤𝐀−𝐜⊤)𝐱=0(\bm{y}^\top \bm{A} - \bm{c}^\top)\bm{x} = 0. Thus 𝐲⊤𝐛=𝐜⊤𝐱\bm{y}^\top\bm{b} = \bm{c}^\top\bm{x}, and by strong duality, the two solutions are optimal.

Conversely, if the two solutions are optimal, it must hold, by strong duality, that 𝐲⊤𝐛=𝐜⊤𝐱\bm{y}^\top \bm{b} = \bm{c}^\top \bm{x} and hence that (𝐲⊤𝐀−𝐜⊤)𝐱=0(\bm{y}^\top \bm{A} - \bm{c}^\top)\bm{x} = 0. Since each component of 𝐱\bm{x} is nonnegative and each component of 𝐲⊤𝐀−𝐜⊤\bm{y}^\top\bm{A} - \bm{c}^\top is nonpositive, the conditions 1.1. and 22. must hold.
