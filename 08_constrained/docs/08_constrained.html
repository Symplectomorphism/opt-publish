Constraints and the Tangent Plane

Constraints

General nonlinear programming problems are of the form

minimizef(𝐱)subject to𝐡(𝐱)=𝟎,𝐠(𝐱)≥𝟎,𝐱∈Ω. \begin{align} \operatorname{minimize} & f(\bm{x}) \\ \text{subject to} & \bm{h}(\bm{x}) = \bm{0}, \;\; \bm{g}(\bm{x}) \geq \bm{0}, \\ & \bm{x} \in \Omega. \end{align}

  • An inequality constraint is said to be active at 𝐱\bm{x} if gi(𝐱)=0g_i(\bm{x}) = 0.
  • It is said to be inactive if gi(𝐱)>0g_i(\bm{x}) > 0.
  • Any equality constraint hi(𝐱)=0h_i(\bm{x}) = 0 is active.
  • In the figure, g1g_1 is active, g2g_2 and g3g_3 are not.
  • If it were known a priori which constraints were active at an optimal solution then it would be a local minimum point of the problem defined by ignoring the inactive constraints.
  • 𝐡=(h1,h2,…,hm)\bm{h} = (h_1, h_2, \ldots, h_m), 𝐠=(g1,g2,…,gp)\;\;\bm{g} = (g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_p) are functional constraints.
  • 𝐱∈Ω\bm{x} \in \Omega: set constraint.
  • We will therefore start by ignoring the inequality constraints and come back to them later.

Tangent Plane

  • The equality constraints define a (hyper)surface S={𝐱:h1(𝐱)=h2(𝐱)=⋯=hm(𝐱)=0}S = \{\bm{x}: h_1(\bm{x}) = h_2(\bm{x}) = \cdots = h_m(\bm{x}) = 0\} of ℝn\mathbb{R}^n.
    • This hypersurface is of dimension n−mn-m (subject to a regularity assumption).
    • If the functions hih_i are continuously differentiable, the surface is said to be smooth.
  • Associated with a point on a smooth surface is the tangent plane at that point.
    • A curve on a surface SS is a familty of points 𝐱(t)∈S\bm{x}(t) \in S, a≤t≤ba \leq t \leq b.
    • The curve is differentiable if 𝐱̇(t)=ddt𝐱(t)\dot{\bm{x}}(t) = \frac{d}{d t}\bm{x}(t) exists, and is twice differentiable if 𝐱̈(t)\ddot{\bm{x}}(t) exists.
    • A curve 𝐱(t)\bm{x}(t) is said to pass through the point 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast if 𝐱*=𝐱(t*)\bm{x}^\ast = \bm{x}(t^\ast) for some a≤t*≤ba \leq t^\ast \leq b.

Tangent Plane

Consider all differentiable curves on SS passing through a point 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast. The tangent plane Tx*ST_{x^\ast}S at 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast of SS is defined as the collection of hte derivatives at 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast of all these differentiable curves.

If 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast is a regular point (to be defined) then we can make the following identification:

T𝐱*S=M≜{𝐝:∇𝐡(𝐱*)𝐝=𝟎}. T_{\bm{x}^\ast}S = M \triangleq \{\bm{d}: \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{d} = \bm{0} \}.

Tangent Plane

Definition (Regular Point)

A point 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast satisfying the constraint 𝐡(𝐱*)=𝟎\bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{0} is said to be a regular point of the constraint if the gradient vectors ∇h1(𝐱*),∇h2(𝐱*),…,∇hm(𝐱*)\nabla h_1(\bm{x}^\ast), \nabla h_2(\bm{x}^\ast), \ldots, \nabla h_m(\bm{x}^\ast) are linearly independent.

 

 

First-Order Necessary Conditions

First-Order Necessary Conditions

Lemma

Let 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast be a regular point of the constraints 𝐡(𝐱)=𝟎\bm{h}(\bm{x}) = \bm{0} and a local extremum point of ff subject to these constraints. Then for all 𝐝∈ℝn\bm{d} \in \mathbb{R}^n, we have ∇𝐡(𝐱*)𝐝=𝟎⇒∇f(𝐱*)𝐝=0. \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} = \bm{0} \;\; \Rightarrow \;\; \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{d} = 0.

Proof

Let 𝐝∈T𝐱*S\bm{d} \in T_{\bm{x}^\ast}S and let 𝐱(t)∈S\bm{x}(t) \in S such that 𝐱(0)=𝐱*\bm{x}(0) = \bm{x}^\ast and 𝐱̇(0)=𝐝\dot{\bm{x}}(0) = \bm{d} for −a≤t≤a-a \leq t \leq a for some a>0a > 0.

Since 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast is a constrained local minimum point of ff, we have

ddtf(𝐱(t))|t=0=∇f(𝐱*)𝐝=0. \frac{d}{dt}f(\bm{x}(t))\Bigg\rvert_{t=0} = \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} = 0.

This lemma says that ∇f(𝐱*)⊥T𝐱*S\quad \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \perp T_{\bm{x}^\ast}S.

Theorem (FONC)

Let 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast be a regular local minimum point of ff subject to the constraint 𝐡(𝐱)=𝟎\bm{h}(\bm{x}) = \bm{0}. Then there is a 𝛌∈ℝm\bm{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^m such that

∇f(𝐱*)−𝛌⊤∇𝐡(𝐱*)=𝟎.(1) \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{0}. \qquad(1)

Proof

From the lemma, we may conclude that the linear system

∇f(𝐱*)𝐝≠0,and∇𝐡(𝐱*)𝐝=𝟎 \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} \neq 0, \;\; \text{and} \;\; \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{d} = \bm{0}

Has no feasible solution 𝐝\bm{d}. Then, by Farkas’s lemma, its alternative system must have a solution. Specifically, there is a 𝛌∈ℝm\bm{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^m such that ∇f(𝐱*)−𝛌⊤∇𝐡(𝐱*)=𝟎\nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{0}.

The FONC Equation 1 together with the constraints 𝐡(𝐱*)=𝟎\bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{0} give a total of n+mn+m equations in the n+mn+m variables comprising 𝐱*,𝛌\bm{x}^\ast, \bm{\lambda}.

Lagrangian

  • Introduce the Lagrangian associated with the constrained problem, defined as

ℓ(𝐱,𝛌)=f(𝐱)−𝛌⊤𝐡(𝐱).(2) \ell(\bm{x}, \bm{\lambda}) = f(\bm{x}) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \bm{h}(\bm{x}). \qquad(2)

  • The FONC can then be expressed as the Lagrangian derivatives

∇𝐱ℓ(𝐱,𝛌)=𝟎,∇𝛌ℓ(𝐱,𝛌)=𝟎.(3) \nabla_{\bm{x}} \ell(\bm{x}, \bm{\lambda}) = \bm{0}, \qquad \nabla_{\bm{\lambda}} \ell(\bm{x}, \bm{\lambda}) = \bm{0}. \qquad(3)

  • The Lagrangian can be viewed as a combined objective function with a penalized term on the cosntraint violations.
    • Each λi\lambda_i is the penalty weight on equality constraint hi(𝐱)=0h_i(\bm{x}) = 0.
    • With appropriate λi\lambda_i’s, a constrained problem could then be solved as an unconstrained optimization problem.
    • If ff is convex and 𝐡(𝐱)\bm{h}(\bm{x}) is affine 𝐀𝐱−𝐛\bm{Ax} - \bm{b}, then ℓ(⋅)\ell(\cdot) is convex in 𝐱\bm{x} for every fixed 𝛌\bm{\lambda}.

Theorem

The first-order necessary conditions are sufficient if ff is convex and 𝐡\bm{h} is affine.

Sensitivity

  • The Lagrange multipliers associated with a constrained minimization problem have an interpretation as prices, similar to the prices in LP.

  • Let a minimal solution 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast be a regular point and 𝛌*\bm{\lambda}^\ast be the corresponding Lagrange multiplier vector. Consider the family of problems

z(𝐛)=minimizef(𝐱)1234subject to𝐡(𝐱)=𝐛,𝐛∈ℝm.(4) \begin{align} z(\bm{b}) = &\operatorname{minimize} &f(\bm{x}) \phantom{1234} & \\ & \text{subject to} & \bm{h}(\bm{x}) = \bm{b}, & \bm{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m. \end{align} \qquad(4)

  • For sufficiently small |𝐛||\bm{b}|, the problem will have a solution point 𝐱(𝐛)\bm{x}(\bm{b}) near 𝐱(𝟎)=𝐱*\bm{x}(\bm{0}) = \bm{x}^\ast.
    • For each of these solutions, there is a corresponding minimum value z(𝐛)=f(𝐱(𝐛))z(\bm{b}) = f(\bm{x}(\bm{b})).
    • The components of the gradient of this function can be regarded as the incremental rate of change in value per unit change in the constraint requirement.

Sensitivity Theorem

Consider the family of problems Equation 4. Suppose that for every 𝐛∈ℝm\bm{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m in a region containing 𝟎\bm{0}, its minimizer 𝐱(𝐛)\bm{x}(\bm{b}) is continuously differentiable depending on 𝐛\bm{b}. Let 𝐱*=𝐱(𝟎)\bm{x}^\ast = \bm{x}(\bm{0}) with the corresponding Lagrange multiplier 𝛌*\bm{\lambda}^\ast. Then

∇z(𝟎)=∇𝐛f(𝐱(𝐛))|𝐛=𝟎=(𝛌*)⊤. \nabla z(\bm{0}) = \nabla_\bm{b} f(\bm{x}(\bm{b})) \Bigg\rvert_{\bm{b}=\bm{0}} = \left(\bm{\lambda}^\ast\right)^\top.

Proof

Using the chain rule and taking derivatives with respect to 𝐛\bm{b} on both sides of

𝐛=𝐡(𝐱(𝐛)) \bm{b} = \bm{h}(\bm{x}(\bm{b}))

at 𝐛=𝟎\bm{b} = \bm{0}, we have

𝐈=∇𝐛𝐡(𝐱(𝐛))|𝐛=𝟎=∇𝐱𝐡(𝐱(𝟎))∇𝐛𝐱(𝟎)=∇𝐱𝐡(𝐱*)∇𝐛𝐱(𝟎). \bm{I} = \nabla_\bm{b} \bm{h}(\bm{x}(\bm{b})) \Bigg\rvert_{\bm{b}=\bm{0}} = \nabla_\bm{x} \bm{h}(\bm{x}(\bm{0}))\nabla_\bm{b}\bm{x}(\bm{0}) = \nabla_\bm{x}\bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast)\nabla_\bm{b}\bm{x}(\bm{0}).

On the other hand, using the chain rule and the first-order condition for 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast and the above matrix equality

∇𝐛f(𝐱(𝐛))|𝐛=𝟎=∇f(𝐱(𝟎))∇𝐛𝐱(𝟎)=∇f(𝐱*)∇𝐛𝐱(𝟎)=(𝛌*)⊤∇𝐱𝐡(𝐱*)∇𝐛𝐱(𝟎)=(𝛌*)⊤. \nabla_\bm{b} f(\bm{x}(\bm{b})) \Bigg\rvert_{\bm{b}=\bm{0}} = \nabla f(\bm{x}(\bm{0})) \nabla_{\bm{b}}\bm{x}(\bm{0}) = \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \nabla_{\bm{b}}\bm{x}(\bm{0}) = \left(\bm{\lambda}^\ast\right)^\top \nabla_\bm{x} \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) \nabla_\bm{b} \bm{x}(\bm{0}) = \left(\bm{\lambda}^\ast\right)^\top.

Theorem (SONC)

Suppose that 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast is a regular local minimum of ff subject to 𝐡(𝐱)=𝟎\bm{h}(\bm{x}) = \bm{0}. Then there is a 𝛌∈ℝm\bm{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^m such that ∇f(𝐱*)−𝛌⊤∇𝐡(𝐱*)=𝟎.(5) \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{0}. \qquad(5) If we denote by MM, the tangent plane, then the matrix 𝐋(𝐱*)=𝐅(𝐱*)−𝛌⊤𝐇(𝐱*)≽𝟎(6) \bm{L}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{F}(\bm{x}^\ast) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \bm{H}(\bm{x}^\ast) \succeq \bm{0} \qquad(6) on MM, that is, 𝐝⊤𝐋(𝐱*)𝐝≥𝟎\bm{d}^\top \bm{L}(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} \geq \bm{0}, ∀𝐝∈M\forall \bm{d} \in M.

Proof

From elementary calculus for every twice differentiable curve 𝐱(t)∈S\bm{x}(t) \in S through 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast we have 0≤d2dt2f(𝐱(t))|t=0=𝐱̇(0)⊤𝐅(𝐱*)𝐱̇(0)+∇f(𝐱*)𝐱̈(0). 0 \leq \frac{d^2}{dt^2}f(\bm{x}(t)) \Bigg\rvert_{t=0} = \dot{\bm{x}}(0)^\top \bm{F}(\bm{x}^\ast) \dot{\bm{x}}(0) + \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \ddot{\bm{x}}(0). Furthermore, differentiating the relation 𝛌⊤𝐡(𝐱(t))=0\bm{\lambda}^\top \bm{h}(\bm{x}(t)) = 0 twice, we obtain 𝐱̇(0)⊤𝛌⊤𝐇(𝐱*)𝐱̇(0)−𝛌⊤∇𝐡(𝐱*)𝐱̈(0)=0. \dot{\bm{x}}(0)^\top \bm{\lambda}^\top \bm{H}(\bm{x}^\ast)\dot{\bm{x}}(0) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) \ddot{\bm{x}}(0) = 0. Additing these two equations yields the result d2dt2f(𝐱(t))|t=0=𝐱̇(0)⊤𝐋(𝐱*)𝐱̇(0)≥0. \frac{d^2}{dt^2}f(\bm{x}(t)) \Bigg\rvert_{t=0} = \dot{\bm{x}}(0)^\top \bm{L}(\bm{x}^\ast) \dot{\bm{x}}(0) \geq 0. Since 𝐱̇(0)\dot{\bm{x}}(0) is arbitrary in MM, we have the stated conclusion.

Suppose there is a point 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast satisfying 𝐡(𝐱*)=𝟎\bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) = \bm{0}, and a 𝛌\bm{\lambda} such that Equation 5 holds. We have

0=hi(𝐲k)=hi(𝐱*)+δk∇hi(𝐱*)𝐬k+δk22𝐬k⊤∇2hi(𝛈i)𝐬k(7) 0 = h_i(\bm{y}_k) = h_i(\bm{x}^\ast) + \delta_k \nabla h_i(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{s}_k + \frac{\delta_k^2}{2}\bm{s}_k^\top \nabla^2 h_i(\bm{\eta}_i) \bm{s}_k \qquad(7) 0≥f(𝐲k)−f(𝐱*)=δk∇f(𝐱*)𝐬k+δk22𝐬k⊤∇2f(𝛈0)𝐬k(8) 0 \geq f(\bm{y}_k) - f(\bm{x}^\ast) = \delta_k \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{s}_k + \frac{\delta_k^2}{2}\bm{s}_k^\top \nabla^2 f(\bm{\eta}_0) \bm{s}_k \qquad(8)

Eigenvalues in the Tangent Subspace

  • Given any vector 𝐝∈M\bm{d} \in M, the vector 𝐋𝐝∈ℝn\bm{Ld} \in \mathbb{R}^n, but not necessarily in MM.
  • We project 𝐋𝐝\bm{Ld} orthogonally back onto MM as in figure.
    • This is the restriction of 𝐋\bm{L} to MM operating on 𝐝\bm{d}.
    • In this way, we obtain a linear transformation 𝐋M:M→M\bm{L}_M: M \rightarrow M.
  • A vector 𝐲∈M\bm{y} \in M is an eigenvector of 𝐋M\bm{L}_M if ∃λ\exists \lambda s.t. 𝐋M𝐲=λ𝐲\bm{L}_M\bm{y} = \lambda \bm{y} (λ\lambda: eigenvalue of 𝐋M\bm{L}_M).
    • In terms of 𝐋\bm{L}, we see that 𝐲\bm{y} is an eigenvector of 𝐋M\bm{L}_M if 𝐋𝐲\bm{Ly} can be written as a sum of λ𝐲\lambda \bm{y} and a vector orthogonal to MM.
  • Introduce an orthonormal basis {𝐞1,…,𝐞n−m}\{\bm{e}_1, \ldots, \bm{e}_{n-m}\} of MM.
    • Define 𝐄≜[𝐞1𝐞2⋯𝐞n−m]\bm{E} \triangleq \begin{bmatrix} \bm{e}_1 & \bm{e}_2 & \cdots \bm{e}_{n-m} \end{bmatrix}.
    • Any vector 𝐲∈M\bm{y} \in M can be written as 𝐲=𝐄𝐳\bm{y} = \bm{Ez} for some 𝐳∈ℝn−m\bm{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{n-m}.
    • 𝐋𝐄𝐳\bm{LEz} represents the action of LL on such a vector.


  • To project the result back to MM and express the result back in terms of the basis {𝐞1,𝐞2,…,𝐞n−m}\{\bm{e}_1, \bm{e}_2, \ldots, \bm{e}_{n-m}\}, we multiply by 𝐄⊤\bm{E}^\top: 𝐄⊤𝐋𝐄\bm{E}^\top \bm{LE} is the matrix representation of 𝐋\bm{L} restricted to MM.

Projected Hessians

  • Alternatively, we can construct matrices and determinants of order nn rather than n−mn-m.

  • For simplicity, let 𝐀=∇𝐡\bm{A} = \nabla \bm{h}, which has full row rank.

  • Any 𝐱\bm{x} satisfying 𝐀𝐱=𝟎\bm{Ax} = \bm{0} can be expressed as 𝐱=(𝐈−𝐀⊤(𝐀𝐀⊤)−1𝐀)𝐳≜𝐏𝐀𝐳,𝐳∈ℝn. \bm{x} = (\bm{I} - \bm{A}^\top(\bm{AA}^\top)^{-1}\bm{A})\bm{z} \triangleq \bm{P}_{\bm{A}}\bm{z}, \qquad \bm{z} \in \mathbb{R}^n.

    • If 𝐱⊤𝐋𝐱≥0,∀𝐱∈M\bm{x}^\top \bm{L}\bm{x} \geq 0, \;\; \forall \bm{x} \in M, then 𝐳⊤𝐏𝐀𝐋𝐏𝐀𝐳≥0,∀𝐳∈ℝn\bm{z}^\top \bm{P}_\bm{A}\bm{LP}_\bm{A}\bm{z} \geq 0, \;\; \forall \bm{z} \in \mathbb{R}^n or the matrix 𝐏𝐀𝐋𝐏𝐀≽𝟎\bm{P}_\bm{A}\bm{L}\bm{P}_\bm{A} \succeq \bm{0}.

Projected Hessian Test

The matrix 𝐋\bm{L} is positive definite on MM iff the projected Hessian matrix to MM is positive semidefinite with rank n−mn-m.

In the previous example we had 𝐀=∇𝐡=[100]\bm{A} = \nabla \bm{h} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. Hence

and let JJ be the set of indices jj for which gj(𝐱*)=0g_j(\bm{x}^\ast) = 0. and suppose 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast is a regular point for the constraints. Then there is a vector 𝛌∈ℝm\bm{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^m and a vector 𝛍∈ℝp\bm{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^p with 𝛍≥𝟎\bm{\mu} \geq \bm{0} such that

∇f(𝐱*)−𝛌⊤∇𝐡(𝐱*)−𝛍⊤∇𝐠(𝐱*)=𝟎,𝛍⊤𝐠(𝐱*)=0.(10) \begin{align} \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast) - \bm{\mu}^\top \nabla \bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast) &= \bm{0}, \\ \bm{\mu}^\top \bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast) = 0. \end{align} \qquad(10)

Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) Conditions

Proof

Since 𝛍≥𝟎\bm{\mu} \geq \bm{0} and 𝐠(𝐱*)≥𝟎\bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast) \geq \bm{0}, the second of Equation 10 is equivalent to the statement that a component of 𝛍\bm{\mu} may be nonzero only if the corresponding constraint is active. This is a complementary slackness condition studied in LP, which states that 𝐠(𝐱*)j>0\bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast)_j > 0 implies μj=0\mu_j = 0 and μj=0\mu_j = 0 implies 𝐠(𝐱*)j=0\bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast)_j = 0.

Since 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast is a relative minimum point over the constraint set, it is also a relative minimum over the subset of that set defined by setting the active constraints to zero. Thus, for the resulting equality constrained problem, defined in a nbhd. of 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast, there are Lagrange multipliers. Therefore, we conclude that first of Equation 10 holds with μj=0\mu_j = 0 if gj(𝐱*)≠0g_j(\bm{x}^\ast) \neq 0.

It remains to be shown that 𝛍≥𝟎\bm{\mu} \geq \bm{0}. Suppose μk<0\mu_k < 0 for some k∈Jk \in J. Let S′S' and M′M' be the surface and the tangent plane, resp., defined by all other active constraints at 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast. By the regularity assumption, there is a 𝐝\bm{d} such that 𝐝∈M′\bm{d} \in M', that is, ∇𝐡(𝐱*)𝐝=𝟎\nabla \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{d} = \bm{0} and ∇gj(𝐱*)𝐝=0\nabla g_j(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} = 0 for all j∈Jj \in J but j≠kj \neq k, and ∇gk(𝐱*)𝐝>0\nabla g_k(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} > 0. Multiplying this 𝐝\bm{d} from the right to the first of Equation 10, we have

∇f(𝐱*)𝐝−μk∇gk(𝐱*)𝐝=0or∇f(𝐱*)𝐝=μk∇gk(𝐱*)𝐝<0, \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} - \mu_k \nabla g_k(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} = \mu_k \nabla g_k(\bm{x}^\ast) \bm{d} < 0,

which implies that 𝐝\bm{d} is a descent direction for the objective function.

Let 𝐱(t)∈S′\bm{x}(t) \in S' with 𝐱(0)=𝐱*\bm{x}(0) = \bm{x}^\ast and 𝐱̇(0)=𝐝\dot{\bm{x}}(0) = \bm{d}. Then for small t≥0t \geq 0, 𝐱(t)\bm{x}(t) is feasible – it remains on the surface of S′S' and gk(𝐱(t))>0g_k(\bm{x}(t)) > 0 because ∇gk(𝐱*)𝐝>0\nabla g_k(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{d} > 0 (that is, constrant gkg_k becomes inactive). But

dfdt(𝐱(t))|t=0=∇f(𝐱*)𝐝<𝟎 \frac{df}{dt}(\bm{x}(t))\Bigg\rvert_{t=0} = \nabla f(\bm{x}^\ast)\bm{d} < \bm{0}

which contradicts the minimality of 𝐱(0)=𝐱*\bm{x}(0) = \bm{x}^\ast.

The Lagrangian and First-Order Conditions

Introduce the Lagrangian associated with the problem, defined as

ℓ(𝐱,𝛌,𝛍)=f(𝐱)−𝛌⊤𝐡(𝐱)−𝛍⊤𝐠(𝐱).(11) \ell(\bm{x}, \bm{\lambda}, \bm{\mu}) = f(\bm{x}) - \bm{\lambda}^\top \bm{h}(\bm{x}) - \bm{\mu}^\top \bm{g}(\bm{x}). \qquad(11)

  • The Lagrangian can again be viewed as an uncsontrained objective function combined with the original objective with two penalized terms on constraint violations.
    • λi\lambda_i is the penalty weight on the equality hi(𝐱)=0h_i(\bm{x}) = 0
    • μj\mu_j is the penalty weight on the inequality gj(𝐱)g_j(\bm{x}).
      • There should be no penalty if gj(𝐱)>0g_j(\bm{x}) > 0, so that μj=0\mu_j = 0,
      • Otherwise, μj\mu_j needs to be increased to a positive value in the Lagrangian to pump up the value of gj(𝐱)g_j(\bm{x}) when the Lagrangian is minimized.

First-Order Necessary Conditions

  • (OVC) The original variable constraints of the problem Equation 9.
  • (MSC) The multiplier sign constraints: 𝛌\bm{\lambda} “free” and 𝛍≥𝟎\bm{\mu} \geq \bm{0}. In general, the sign of the multiplier is determined by the sense of the original constraint: (i) if it is == then the sign is “free”, (ii) if it is ≤\leq or ≥\geq then the sign is ≤\leq or ≥\geq, resp.
  • (LDC) The Lagrangian derivative condition: the first of Equation 10
  • (CSC) The complementarity slackness condition: the second of Equation 10

Example



maximize(x1−1)2+(x2−1)2subject to1−x12−x22≥0. \begin{align} \operatorname{maximize} & (x_1 - 1)^2 + (x_2 - 1)^2 \\ \text{subject to} & 1 - x_1^2 - x_2^2 \geq 0. \end{align}

The Lagrangian and the (LDC) conditions are ℓ(x1,x2,μ(≥0))=(x1−1)2+(x2−1)2123−μ(1−x12−x22),(LDC)∇𝐱ℓ(x1,x2,μ)=(2x1(1+μ)−22x2(1+μ)−2)=𝟎, \begin{align} \ell(x_1, x_2, \mu(\geq 0)) &= (x_1 - 1)^2 + (x_2 - 1)^2 \\ & \phantom{123} - \mu(1 - x_1^2 - x_2^2), \\ (\text{LDC}) \;\; \nabla_\bm{x}\ell(x_1, x_2, \mu) &= \begin{pmatrix} 2x_1 (1+\mu) - 2 \\ 2x_2(1+\mu) - 2 \end{pmatrix} = \bm{0}, \end{align}

and the (CSC) condition is μ(1−x12−x22)=0\mu(1 - x_1^2 - x_2^2) = 0.

  • From the two equations of (LDC) and μ≥0\mu \geq 0, we conclude x1=x2x_1 = x_2.
  • We first try μ=0\mu = 0, which, from the two eqns. of (LDC) leads to x1=x2=1x_1 = x_2 = 1 and violates the inequality constraint.
  • Thus the constraint must be active, which gives rise to two possible solutions (x1=x2=12)and(x1=x2=−12). ( x_1 = x_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} ) \;\; \text{and} \;\; (x_1 = x_2 = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}}).
  • The former, again from (LDC), makes μ=2−1\mu = \sqrt{2} - 1; while the latter makes μ=−2−1\mu = -\sqrt{2} - 1, which violates (MSC).
  • Thus, the only qualified first-order solution is x1=x2=12x_1 = x_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, with the corresponding μ=2−1\mu = \sqrt{2} - 1.

Convex Problems

If ff is convex and 𝐡(𝐱)\bm{h}(\bm{x}) is affine 𝐀𝐱−𝐛\bm{Ax} - \bm{b}, and $() are concave functions, then ℓ(⋅)\ell(\cdot) is convex in 𝐱\bm{x} for every fixed 𝛌\bm{\lambda} and 𝛍(≥𝟎)\bm{\mu} (\geq \bm{0}).

Therefore if 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast meets the first of Equation 10, then 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast is the global minimizer of the unconstrained ℓ(𝐱,𝛌,𝛍)\ell(\bm{x}, \bm{\lambda}, \bm{\mu}) with the same 𝛌\bm{\lambda} and 𝛍\bm{\mu}.

Theorem

The FONC are sufficient if ff is convex, 𝐡\bm{h} is affine, and gj(𝐱)g_j(\bm{x}) is concave for all jj.

Proof

Let 𝐱\bm{x} be any feasible solution and 𝐱*\bm{x}^\ast, together with 𝛌*\bm{\lambda}^\ast and 𝛍*\bm{\mu}^\ast satisfy the FONC. Then we have

0≤ℓ(𝐱,𝛌*,𝛍*)−ℓ(𝐱*,𝛌*,𝛍*)=f(𝐱)−f(𝐱*)−(𝛌*)⊤(𝐡(𝐱)−𝐡(𝐱*))−(𝛍*)⊤(𝐠(𝐱)−𝐠(𝐱*))=f(𝐱)−f(𝐱*)−(𝛍*)⊤(𝐠(𝐱)−𝐠(𝐱*))=f(𝐱)−f(𝐱*)−∑j∈Jμj(gj(𝐱)−gj(𝐱*))=f(𝐱)−f(𝐱*)−∑j∈Jμjgj(𝐱)≤f(𝐱)−f(𝐱*). \begin{align} 0 &\leq \ell(\bm{x}, \bm{\lambda}^\ast, \bm{\mu}^\ast) - \ell(\bm{x}^\ast, \bm{\lambda}^\ast, \bm{\mu}^\ast) = f(\bm{x}) - f(\bm{x}^\ast) - (\bm{\lambda}^\ast)^\top (\bm{h}(\bm{x}) - \bm{h}(\bm{x}^\ast)) - (\bm{\mu}^\ast)^\top (\bm{g}(\bm{x}) - \bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast)) \\ &= f(\bm{x}) - f(\bm{x}^\ast) - (\bm{\mu}^\ast)^\top (\bm{g}(\bm{x}) - \bm{g}(\bm{x}^\ast)) = f(\bm{x}) - f(\bm{x}^\ast) - \sum_{j \in J} \mu_j (g_j(\bm{x}) - g_j(\bm{x}^\ast)) \\ &= f(\bm{x}) - f(\bm{x}^\ast) - \sum_{j \in J} \mu_j g_j(\bm{x}) \leq f(\bm{x}) - f(\bm{x}^\ast). \end{align}

which completes the proof.
